heading: The Double-Edged Sword
intro: AI is simultaneously a capability multiplier and a trust liability.

behavioral_label: Behavioral
behavioral_heading_1: Psychological factors
behavioral_heading_2: of AI adoption

behavioral_item1_title: Control
behavioral_item1_desc: People naturally desire control over their environments, which can be stripped when AI cedes responsibility for their tasks. This lack of autonomy can be addressed through delegating the least meaningful or enjoyable tasks to AI systems or customizing them. 

behavioral_item2_title: Comfort
behavioral_item2_desc: People aim to exert the lowest mental energy when doing a task, and thrive in comfortable workflows. When AI has the prospect of creating more work for an employee due to poor output quality, they resist. By understanding that AI is not perfect and learning to prompt more efficiently, employees can increase their comfort level with AI and reduce cleanup work. 


behavioral_item3_title: Identity
behavioral_item3_desc: As one’s career may be a big part of their life, people commonly associate their work with their personal identity. With AI in the picture, this can incite the fear of replacement, causing employees to resist AI because they see it as a competitor rather than a partner. One can address this barrier by highlighting the human-led responsibilities of their role and building AI literacy for the future. 


behavioral_item4_title: Understanding
behavioral_item4_desc: As the brain perceives a lack of information as a threat, humans feel fear and anxiety when faced with the unknown. AI’s ‘black box’ nature of internal, hidden decision-making mechanisms may lead to employee distrust in AI, because they don’t know or understand how it works. Employees can try to understand AI more by observing its patterns, suchas, what tasks an AI model can and can’t handle, and how those tasks differ.
